### 1. Geometric Intuition: Scaling Factors

A determinant is a single number that describes how a linear transformation affects a region.

- **Area and Volume**: In 2D, the determinant measures the factor by which an area is stretched or squeezed. In 3D, it measures the scaling of volume.
- **Interpreting the Value**:
    - **$\det(A) > 1$**: The region is **stretched**.
    - **$0 < \det(A) < 1$**: The region is **squeezed**.
    - **$\det(A) = 0$**: The matrix is **singular** (not invertible); it collapses the space into a lower dimension (e.g., a plane into a line), leaving it with zero area/volume.
    - **$\det(A) < 0$**: The **orientation** of the space is flipped (mirrored).

### 2. The Three Methods of Calculation

You are expected to know these three specific ways to find a determinant:

1. **Pivot Formula**: After performing elimination to get an upper triangular matrix $U$, the determinant is the **product of the pivots**. If you swapped rows, you must account for the sign change.
2. **The Big Formula**: This involves summing $n!$ permutations of matrix entries. Each term is a product of $n$ entries, one from each row and column, multiplied by $+1$ or $-1$ depending on whether the permutation is even or odd.
3. **Co-Factor Method**: You expand along any row or column. The determinant is the sum of entries multiplied by their respective cofactors ($C_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j} \det(\text{minor matrix})$).

### 3. Key Properties for Exam Shortcuts

These properties often allow you to solve determinant problems without heavy calculation:

- **Identity**: $\det(I) = 1$.
- **Transposes**: $\det(A^T) = \det(A)$.
- **Multiplication**: $\det(AB) = \det(A) \cdot \det(B)$.
- **Inverses**: $\det(A^{-1}) = 1/\det(A)$.
- **Row Operations**:
    - **Swapping** two rows reverses the sign.
    - **Multiplying one row** by $t$ multiplies the determinant by $t$.
    - **Multiplying the entire matrix** $nA$ results in $n^k \det(A)$ (where $k$ is the dimension).
    - **Adding a multiple** of one row to another does **not** change the determinant.

### 4. Assignment Solutions (Section 3)

#### **3.1 The Big Formula***

To find the determinant of the 4x4 matrix, we identify non-zero permutations (terms with exactly one entry from each row and column). Matrix entries: $a_{11}=2, a_{14}=-5, a_{21}=3, a_{23}=1, a_{32}=4, a_{33}=-2, a_{42}=-3, a_{44}=5$.

There are only **two non-zero terms**:

1. **Term 1**: $(a_{11})(a_{23})(a_{32})(a_{44}) = (2)(1)(4)(5) = 40$.
    - Column sequence: $(1, 3, 2, 4)$. This requires **1 swap** to reach $(1, 2, 3, 4)$, making the sign **negative**.
    - Result: **$-40$**.
2. **Term 2**: $(a_{14})(a_{21})(a_{33})(a_{42}) = (-5)(3)(-2)(-3) = -90$.
    - Column sequence: $(4, 1, 3, 2)$. This requires **2 swaps** ($(4,1) \to (1,4,3,2) \to (1,2,3,4)$), making the sign **positive**.
    - Result: **$-90$**.

- **Total $\det(A)$**: $-40 - 90 = \mathbf{-130}$.

#### **3.2 CoFactor Method***

Given $A = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & -2 & 5 \ 0 & 3 & 4 \ -1 & 6 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

**i) Determinant**: Expand along Row 2 (due to the zero): $\det(A) = -0(C_{21}) + 3 \begin{vmatrix} 7 & 5 \ -1 & 2 \end{vmatrix} - 4 \begin{vmatrix} 7 & -2 \ -1 & 6 \end{vmatrix}$ $\det(A) = 3(14 + 5) - 4(42 - 2) = 3(19) - 4(40) = 57 - 160 = \mathbf{-103}$.

**ii) False Expansion Theorem**: If we multiply Row 1 entries by the cofactors of Row 2, the sum must be zero.

- Cofactors of Row 2: $C_{21}=34, C_{22}=19, C_{23}=-40$.
- Row 1 entries: $7, -2, 5$.
- **Sum**: $(7)(34) + (-2)(19) + (5)(-40) = 238 - 38 - 200 = \mathbf{0}$. (Proved).

**iii) Inverse ($A^{-1}$)**: Using the formula $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(A)} (Cof(A))^T$: After calculating all cofactors, the inverse is: $$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{-103} \begin{bmatrix} -18 & 34 & -23 \ -4 & 19 & -28 \ 3 & -40 & 21 \end{bmatrix}$$
