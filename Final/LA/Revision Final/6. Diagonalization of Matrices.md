### 1. Theoretical Foundation: $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$

Diagonalization allows us to represent a square matrix $A$ in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

- **The Goal**: Find a diagonal matrix $\Lambda$ that is "similar" to $A$.
- **Eigenvector Matrix ($S$)**: The columns of $S$ are the independent eigenvectors of $A$.
- **Eigenvalue Matrix ($\Lambda$)**: A diagonal matrix where the diagonal entries are the eigenvalues corresponding to the eigenvectors in $S$.
- **Requirement**: For a matrix $A$ of size $n \times n$ to be diagonalizable, it must have **$n$ linearly independent eigenvectors**. If $A$ has distinct eigenvalues, it is always diagonalizable.

### 2. Powers of a Matrix

The primary advantage of diagonalization is the ease of calculating $A^k$: $$A^k = S\Lambda^k S^{-1}$$ Because $\Lambda$ is diagonal, $\Lambda^k$ is simply every diagonal entry raised to the power of $k$. This "sweeps away the difficulties" of multiplying a matrix by itself many times.

### 3. Application: Finding the $n$-th Term of a Sequence

Linear sequences (like Fibonacci or Perrin) can be expressed as a matrix equation $u_{k+1} = Au_k$, where the state vector $u_k$ contains the terms of the sequence.

#### **Assignment Solution: Question 6.1 (i) Perrin Sequence***

- **Definition**: $L_n = L_{n-1} + L_{n-2}$ with $L_0 = 2, L_1 = 1$.
- **Matrix Setup**: $$u_{k+1} = \begin{bmatrix} L_{k+1} \ L_k \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} L_k \ L_{k-1} \end{bmatrix} \implies A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad u_0 = \begin{bmatrix} L_1 \ L_0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 2 \end{bmatrix}$$.
- **Step 1: Find Eigenvalues ($\lambda$)**: $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0 \implies \lambda^2 - \lambda - 1 = 0$. Roots: $\lambda_1 = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2}$ (approx 1.618), $\lambda_2 = \frac{1 - \sqrt{5}}{2}$ (approx -0.618).
- **Step 2: General Formula**: The $n$-th term is found using $u_n = A^n u_0 = S\Lambda^n S^{-1} u_0$.
- **Step 3: Verification for 8th term ($L_8$)**:
    - Manual Sequence: $L_0=2, L_1=1, L_2=3, L_3=4, L_4=7, L_5=11, L_6=18, L_7=29, L_8=47$.
    - Diagonalization Result: Calculating $u_8 = A^8 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 2 \end{bmatrix}$ yields $\begin{bmatrix} L_9 \ L_8 \end{bmatrix}$, where **$L_8 = 47$**.

#### **Assignment Solution: Question 6.1 (ii) Padovan Sequence***

- **Definition**: $P_n = P_{n-2} + P_{n-3}$ with $P_0=1, P_1=1, P_2=1$.
- **Matrix Setup**: To relate $P_n$ to previous terms, we use a 3x3 matrix: $$\begin{bmatrix} P_{n+1} \ P_n \ P_{n-1} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} P_n \ P_{n-1} \ P_{n-2} \end{bmatrix} \implies A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$
- Follow the same process: Find the 3 eigenvalues/eigenvectors, set up $A^n = S\Lambda^n S^{-1}$, and calculate $u_8 = A^8 u_0$ to find $P_8$.
