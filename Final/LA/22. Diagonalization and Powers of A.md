## 🔑 Core Concept: Diagonalization for Matrix Powers

Finding the $k$-th power of a matrix $A$ directly via matrix multiplication is computationally expensive and difficult to express as a general formula. 

Instead, we use **diagonalization**. If a matrix $A$ has independent eigenvectors, it can be decomposed into:
$$A = S \Lambda S^{-1}$$
Where:
*   $S$ is the matrix containing the **eigenvectors** as columns.
*   $\Lambda$ is the diagonal matrix containing the **eigenvalues**.
*   $S^{-1}$ is the inverse of the eigenvector matrix.

> [!important] The Power Formula
> When raising $A$ to the power of $k$, the $S^{-1}S$ terms cancel out in the middle:
> $$A^k = (S \Lambda S^{-1})(S \Lambda S^{-1})...(S \Lambda S^{-1}) = S \Lambda^k S^{-1}$$
> Because $\Lambda$ is a diagonal matrix, $\Lambda^k$ is simply found by raising each diagonal element (the eigenvalues) to the power of $k$.

## 📝 Worked Example Problem

**Part 1:** Find a formula for $C^k$ given the matrix:
$$C = \begin{pmatrix} 2b-a & a-b \\ 2b-2a & 2a-b \end{pmatrix}$$
*(Note: This is a 2x2 matrix parameterized by variables $a$ and $b$)*.

**Part 2:** Calculate $C^{100}$ when $a = -1$ and $b = -1$.

---

### Step 1: Find the Eigenvalues ($\lambda$)

To find the eigenvalues, we must solve the characteristic equation: $\det(C - \lambda I) = 0$.

$$C - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} (2b-a)-\lambda & a-b \\ 2b-2a & (2a-b)-\lambda \end{pmatrix}$$

Taking the determinant:
$$\det(C - \lambda I) = [(2b-a)-\lambda][(2a-b)-\lambda] - [(a-b)(2b-2a)] = 0$$

Expanding and simplifying:
1. First term: $\lambda^2 - (2b - a + 2a - b)\lambda + (2b-a)(2a-b)$
2. Simplify middle term: $\lambda^2 - (a+b)\lambda + (4ab - 2b^2 - 2a^2 + ab)$
3. Subtract second term: $-(2ab - 2a^2 - 2b^2 + 2ab)$
4. Combine: $\lambda^2 - (a+b)\lambda + ab = 0$

Factoring the polynomial:
$$(\lambda - a)(\lambda - b) = 0$$

> [!success] Eigenvalues
> $$\lambda_1 = a$$
> $$\lambda_2 = b$$

---

### Step 2: Find the Eigenvectors ($x$)

We find the eigenvectors by finding the nullspace of $(C - \lambda I)$ for each eigenvalue.

**For $\lambda_1 = a$:**
Substitute $\lambda = a$ into $(C - \lambda I)$:
$$C - aI = \begin{pmatrix} 2b-2a & a-b \\ 2b-2a & a-b \end{pmatrix}$$
We want a vector $x_1 = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$ such that $(C - aI)x_1 = 0$. 
Notice the first column is exactly $-2$ times the second column. Therefore, the nullspace vector is:
$$x_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

**For $\lambda_2 = b$:**
Substitute $\lambda = b$ into $(C - \lambda I)$:
$$C - bI = \begin{pmatrix} b-a & a-b \\ 2b-2a & 2a-2b \end{pmatrix}$$
Notice the first column is exactly $-1$ times the second column. Therefore, the nullspace vector is:
$$x_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

---

### Step 3: Construct $S$, $\Lambda$, and $S^{-1}$

Now we build our decomposition matrices.

**The Eigenvector Matrix ($S$):**
$$S = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

**The Eigenvalue Matrix ($\Lambda$):**
$$\Lambda = \begin{pmatrix} a & 0 \\ 0 & b \end{pmatrix}$$

**The Inverse Matrix ($S^{-1}$):**
Recall the formula for a 2x2 inverse: $\frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$
$$\det(S) = (1)(1) - (1)(2) = -1$$
$$S^{-1} = \frac{1}{-1} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

---

### Step 4: Compute $C^k$

Using the formula $C^k = S \Lambda^k S^{-1}$:

$$C^k = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a^k & 0 \\ 0 & b^k \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

First, multiply the last two matrices together:
$$\begin{pmatrix} a^k & 0 \\ 0 & b^k \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -a^k & a^k \\ 2b^k & -b^k \end{pmatrix}$$

Now multiply $S$ by the result:
$$C^k = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} -a^k & a^k \\ 2b^k & -b^k \end{pmatrix}$$

> [!success] Final Formula for $C^k$
> $$C^k = \begin{pmatrix} 2b^k - a^k & a^k - b^k \\ 2b^k - 2a^k & 2a^k - b^k \end{pmatrix}$$

> [!tip] Sanity Check
> A great way to check your work on exams is to plug in $k=1$. 
> $$C^1 = \begin{pmatrix} 2b^1 - a^1 & a^1 - b^1 \\ 2b^1 - 2a^1 & 2a^1 - b^1 \end{pmatrix}$$
> This perfectly matches the original matrix $C$ provided in the problem!

---

### Step 5: Solve Part 2 (Calculate $C^{100}$)

We need to evaluate $C^{100}$ when $a = -1$ and $b = -1$.

Instead of multiplying out the original matrix 100 times, plug these values directly into our new formula.

First, note the powers of our variables:
*   $a^{100} = (-1)^{100} = 1$
*   $b^{100} = (-1)^{100} = 1$

Substitute $a^{100}=1$ and $b^{100}=1$ into the formula:
$$C^{100} = \begin{pmatrix} 2(1) - 1 & 1 - 1 \\ 2(1) - 2(1) & 2(1) - 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$C^{100} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

> [!success] Final Answer
> When $a=-1$ and $b=-1$, $C^{100}$ is simply the Identity Matrix, **$I$**.
