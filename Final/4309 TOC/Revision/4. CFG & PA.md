## 1. The Chomsky Hierarchy (Quick Recall)
*   **Regular (NFA/DFA/Regex)** $\subset$ **Context-Free (PDA/CFG)** $\subset$ **Turing-Decidable** $\subset$ **Turing-Recognizable (TM)**.

## 2. Context-Free Grammars (CFG)
*   **Definition:** $G = (V, \Sigma, R, S)$ $\to$ (Variables, Terminals, Rules, Start).
*   **Rule Format:** $A \to (\text{String of Variables \& Terminals})$.
*   **Ambiguity:** A CFG is ambiguous if *just one string* has **$\ge 2$ distinct parse trees** (or $\ge 2$ leftmost/rightmost derivations).

## 3. Pushdown Automata (PDA)
*   **Concept:** Finite Automaton + **Stack (LIFO)**.
*   **Definition:** $M = (Q, \Sigma, \Gamma, \delta, q_0, F)$ $\to$ $\Gamma$ is the *Stack Alphabet*.
*   **Power:** **Non-deterministic PDAs (NPDA) > Deterministic PDAs (DPDA)**. (e.g., $ww^R$ requires guessing the middle).
*   **Equivalence Theorem:** A language is a CFL $\iff$ some PDA recognizes it.

## 4. Chomsky Normal Form (CNF) - *Guaranteed Exam Question*
> [!warning] Exam Rule
> **ALL** rules must look like: $A \to BC$ (two variables) OR $A \to a$ (one terminal).
> **ORDER OF STEPS MATTERS STRICLY!**

**The 4 Steps to Convert CFG $\to$ CNF:**
1.  **New Start:** Add $S_0 \to S$. (Start var can never be on the right side).
2.  **Eliminate $\epsilon$-rules:** Remove $A \to \epsilon$.
    *   *Patch:* If you had $R \to uAv$, add $R \to uv$. (Do for all combinations if multiple $A$'s exist).
3.  **Eliminate Unit Rules:** Remove $A \to B$.
    *   *Patch:* Look at what $B$ points to ($B \to u$). Add $A \to u$. Repeat until no single variables remain on the right.
4.  **Binarize & Terminals:**
    *   *Too long:* $A \to u_1 u_2 u_3 \dots$ becomes a chain: $A \to u_1 A_1$, $A_1 \to u_2 A_2$, etc.
    *   *Mixed up:* $A \to aB$ becomes $A \to U_a B$ (where $U_a \to a$).

## 5. CYK Algorithm - *Dynamic Programming Parsing*
> [!info] Pre-requisite & Complexity
> **Must be in CNF!**
> **Time:** $\mathcal{O}(n^3 \cdot |G|)$ | **Space:** $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$

**How to build the table $T[i, j]$** (Starting at index $i$, length $j$):
*   **Base Case (Length $j=1$):** Look at the individual characters. Add $A$ if $A \to s_i$.
*   **Recursive Step (Length $j>1$):** Split the substring into all possible $k$ prefixes/suffixes. Look for rule $A \to BC$ where $B$ makes the left part and $C$ makes the right part.
*   **Acceptance Condition:** The string is accepted if the Start Variable **$S$** is in the very top-right cell: **$S \in T[1, n]$**.

## 6. Pumping Lemma for CFLs - *Proof Template*
Use this to prove a language is **NOT** a CFL (usually $0^n1^n2^n$ or $ww$).

> [!abstract] The 3 Conditions to Memorize
> If $s \in A$ and $|s| \ge p$, then $s = uvxyz$ where:
> 1. $uv^i x y^i z \in A$ for all $i \ge 0$ *(You can pump $v$ and $y$)*
> 2. $|vy| \ge 1$ (or $vy \neq \epsilon$) *(At least one pumpable part exists)*
> 3. $|vxy| \le p$ *(The pumpable window is restricted in size)*

**Standard Exam Proof Steps ($L = \{0^n1^n2^n\}$):**
1.  Assume $L$ is a CFL. Let $p$ be the pumping length.
2.  Choose $s = 0^p1^p2^p \in L$.
3.  By Condition 3 ($|vxy| \le p$), the substring $vxy$ can span at most **two** symbol types (e.g., just 0s and 1s). It *cannot* contain 0s, 1s, and 2s simultaneously.
4.  By Condition 1, $uv^2xy^2z \in L$. Pumping $v$ and $y$ increases the count of at most two symbols.
5.  This breaks the equal number of $0$s, $1$s, and $2$s $\to$ resulting string $\notin L$.
6.  **Contradiction.** $L$ is not a CFL.
