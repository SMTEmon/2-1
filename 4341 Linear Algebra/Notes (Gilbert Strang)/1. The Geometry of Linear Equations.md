# The Geometry of Linear Equations

The fundamental problem of linear algebra is to solve a system of $n$ linear equations with $n$ unknowns.

## 1. The Core Example (2 Equations, 2 Unknowns)

We start with a specific system to visualize the concepts:

$$
\begin{cases} 
2x - y = 0 \\ 
-x + 2y = 3 
\end{cases}
$$

This system can be viewed in three distinct ways: **Row Picture**, **Column Picture**, and **Matrix Form**.

### A. The Matrix Form ($A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$)
We separate the coefficients, the unknowns, and the right-hand side.

$$
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}}_{A \text{ (Coefficient Matrix)}} 
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}}_{\mathbf{x} \text{ (Unknowns)}} = 
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}}_{\mathbf{b} \text{ (RHS)}}
$$

### B. The Row Picture (Traditional View)
We treat each row as an individual equation representing a geometric object.
*   **Geometry:** In 2D, each equation represents a **line**.
*   **Goal:** Find the point where the lines intersect.

**Visualizing the Lines:**
1.  $2x - y = 0$ passes through the origin $(0,0)$ and $(1,2)$.
2.  $-x + 2y = 3$ passes through $(-3, 0)$ and $(0, 1.5)$.
   
Ask: Is there a point on the xy plane which lies on both of these lines?

> [!check] Solution
> The lines intersect at exactly one point: **$x=1, y=2$**.

### C. The Column Picture (The "Linear Algebra" View)

> [!important] Key Concept
> instead of looking at rows (equations), look at the **columns** (vectors). We are looking for a **Linear Combination** of the columns to produce the vector $\mathbf{b}$.

Rewrite the system as a vector equation:

$$
x \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}}_{\text{Col } 1} + 
y \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}}_{\text{Col } 2} = 
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}}_{\mathbf{b}}
$$

**Visual Interpretation:**
*   Take the vector $\text{Col}_1$. Scale it by $x$.
*   Take the vector $\text{Col}_2$. Scale it by $y$.
*   Add them "tip-to-tail".
*   **Goal:** Do they land on vector $\mathbf{b}$?

Ask: Is there a linear combination of the vectors $\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$ and $\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$ that direct to the vector $\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}$

**The Solution in the Column Picture:**
We need **1** of Column 1 and **2** of Column 2 to reach $\mathbf{b}$.
$$
1 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} + 2 \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2-2 \\ -1+4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}
$$
This confirms $x=1, y=2$.

## 2. The 3-D Case (3 Equations, 3 Unknowns)

$$
A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}
$$

### Row Picture (Hard to see)
*   Each equation is a **Plane** in 3D space ($x, y, z$).
*   Two planes intersect in a **Line**.
*   The third plane intersects that line at a **Point**.
*   *Conclusion:* Visualizing three intersecting planes is cognitively difficult.

### Column Picture (Clearer)
We invoke the same logic: **Linear Combination of Columns**.

$$
x \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + 
y \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} + 
z \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = 
\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}
$$

**Geometry:** We have 3 directional vectors in 3D space. We stretch and add them to reach point $\mathbf{b}$.

> [!example] Inspection Solution
> By looking at the equation, $\mathbf{b}$ is actually identical to **Column 3**.
> Therefore, we need:
> - 0 of Col 1
> - 0 of Col 2
> - 1 of Col 3
> 
> **Solution:** $\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

## 3. Matrix Multiplication: A New Perspective

The standard way to multiply a matrix by a vector (row $\cdot$ column) gives the numbers, but hides the structure. Strang introduces a fundamental way to view $A\mathbf{x}$.

> [!abstract] Axiom: Matrix $\times$ Vector
> $A\mathbf{x}$ is a **linear combination of the columns of $A$**.

$$
\begin{bmatrix} \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ \mathbf{c_1} & \mathbf{c_2} & \dots & \mathbf{c_n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \end{bmatrix} 
\begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{bmatrix} 
= 
x_1 \mathbf{c_1} + x_2 \mathbf{c_2} + \dots + x_n \mathbf{c_n}
$$

If you interpret $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ this way, you are asking: **"How do I mix the columns of A to produce b?"**

---

## 4. The Big Picture: Solveability

Can we solve $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ for **every** possible vector $\mathbf{b}$?

### In the Language of Columns:
Do the linear combinations of the columns of $A$ fill the entire $n$-dimensional space (e.g., all of $\mathbb{R}^3$)?

| Scenario | Column Geometry | Matrix Status | Solvability |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Columns are Independent** | The vectors go in different directions. They define a full volume. | **Non-Singular** (Invertible) | Solution exists for **every** $\mathbf{b}$. |
| **Columns are Dependent** | One column lies in the plane (or line) of the others. They are "flat". | **Singular** (Not Invertible) | Solution exists **only** if $\mathbf{b}$ lies on that specific plane. |

**Example of Failure (Singular Matrix in 3D):**
If Column 3 = Column 1 + Column 2, then the three vectors lie on a single "sheet" (plane) passing through the origin.
*   We can reach any $\mathbf{b}$ on that sheet.
*   We **cannot** reach any $\mathbf{b}$ that sticks out of the sheet.

> [!summary] Summary of Lecture 1
> 1. **Row Picture:** Intersection of $n$ planes (hard to visualize).
> 2. **Column Picture:** Combination of $n$ vectors (easier to visualize).
> 3. **$A\mathbf{x}$** is a linear combination of the columns of $A$.
> 4. Solving $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ means finding the coefficients $(x,y,z)$ to combine columns to produce $\mathbf{b}$.
