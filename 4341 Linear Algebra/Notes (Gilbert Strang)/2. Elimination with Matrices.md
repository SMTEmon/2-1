## 1. Introduction to Elimination
The primary method for solving linear equations is **Elimination** (Gaussian Elimination). It is more efficient and commonly used by software (like MATLAB) compared to determinants.

**The Goal:** transform the coefficient matrix $A$ into an **Upper Triangular Matrix ($U$)**.

### The Example System
We start with a system of 3 equations and 3 unknowns ($x, y, z$):

$$
\begin{cases}
1x + 2y + 1z = 2 \\
3x + 8y + 1z = 12 \\
0x + 4y + 1z = 2
\end{cases}
$$

In matrix form $Ax = b$:
$$
A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 8 & 1 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
b = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 12 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}
$$

## 2. Forward Elimination Algorithm

The algorithm proceeds column by column to create zeros below the diagonal.

### Step 1: Using the First Pivot
*   **Pivot:** The first non-zero element in row 1, column 1. Here, it is **1**.
*   **Goal:** Eliminate the $3x$ in the second equation (entry $a_{21}$).
*   **The Multiplier:** We need to multiply Row 1 by a number such that when subtracted from Row 2, the $3$ becomes $0$.
    *   $\text{Multiplier} = \frac{\text{Entry to eliminate}}{\text{Pivot}} = \frac{3}{1} = 3$.
*   **Operation:** $\text{Row}_2 = \text{Row}_2 - 3(\text{Row}_1)$.

**Visualizing the Matrix Change:**
$$
\begin{bmatrix} \fbox{1} & 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 8 & 1 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
\xrightarrow{R_2 - 3R_1}
\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & -2 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$
*(Note: The $(3,1)$ entry is already 0, so no operation is needed for Row 3 in this column.)*

### Step 2: Using the Second Pivot
*   **Pivot:** The element in row 2, column 2 of the *new* matrix. Here, it is **2**.
*   **Goal:** Eliminate the $4$ in the third equation (entry $a_{32}$).
*   **The Multiplier:** $\frac{4}{2} = 2$.
*   **Operation:** $\text{Row}_3 = \text{Row}_3 - 2(\text{Row}_2)$.

**Visualizing the Matrix Change:**
$$
\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & \fbox{2} & -2 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
\xrightarrow{R_3 - 2R_2}
\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & -2 \\ 0 & 0 & \fbox{5} \end{bmatrix}
$$

> [!SUCCESS] Target Reached: Matrix $U$
> The final matrix is $U$ (Upper Triangular). The **Pivots** are 1, 2, and 5.
> $$ U = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & -2 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} $$

---

## 3. Failure and Permutations
Elimination usually works, but it can fail if a pivot is zero.

1.  **Temporary Failure (Repairable):** A 0 appears in the pivot position, but there is a non-zero number *below* it in the same column.
    *   **Solution:** Perform a **Row Exchange** (Permutation). Swap the rows to bring a non-zero number into the pivot position.
2.  **Complete Failure (Singular):** A 0 appears in the pivot position, and all numbers below it are also 0.
    *   **Result:** The matrix is not invertible. The system has either no solution or infinite solutions.

## 4. Back Substitution
Once we have $U$, we solve for the variables in reverse order (bottom to top). We must apply the same row operations to the vector $b$ to get vector $c$.

**The Augmented Matrix:** Instead of treating $b$ separately, we tack it onto $A$ as an extra column $[A|b]$.

$$
\left[
\begin{array}{ccc|c}
1 & 2 & 1 & 2 \\
3 & 8 & 1 & 12 \\
0 & 4 & 1 & 2
\end{array}
\right]
\xrightarrow{\text{Elimination}}
\left[
\begin{array}{ccc|c}
1 & 2 & 1 & 2 \\
0 & 2 & -2 & 6 \\
0 & 0 & 5 & -10
\end{array}
\right]
$$

**Solving the system $Ux = c$:**
1.  **Equation 3:** $5z = -10 \implies \mathbf{z = -2}$
2.  **Equation 2:** $2y - 2z = 6 \implies 2y - 2(-2) = 6 \implies 2y + 4 = 6 \implies \mathbf{y = 1}$
3.  **Equation 1:** $1x + 2y + 1z = 2 \implies x + 2(1) + (-2) = 2 \implies \mathbf{x = 2}$

## 5. Elimination Matrices (Elementary Matrices)
We can express row operations as **Matrix Multiplication**.

> [!NOTE] The Golden Rule of Matrix Multiplication Operations
> *   To perform an operation on **Rows**, multiply by a matrix on the **LEFT**.
> *   To perform an operation on **Columns**, multiply by a matrix on the **RIGHT**.

### Constructing Elementary Matrix $E_{21}$
We want a matrix that subtracts 3 * Row 1 from Row 2, while leaving Rows 1 and 3 alone. We start with the **Identity Matrix ($I$)** and apply that specific operation to it.

$$
I = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
\xrightarrow{R_2 - 3R_1}
E_{21} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

**Applying it:**
$$
E_{21} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 8 & 1 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & -2 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

### Constructing Elementary Matrix $E_{32}$
We want to subtract 2 * Row 2 from Row 3. Start with $I$ and apply the operation.

$$
E_{32} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

### The Total Elimination Process
The mathematical expression for elimination is applying these matrices in sequence from the left:
$$ E_{32} (E_{21} A) = U $$

Due to the **Associative Law**, we can group the matrices (but we cannot change the order):
$$ (E_{32} E_{21}) A = U $$

## 6. Permutation Matrices ($P$)
To exchange rows, we multiply by a Permutation Matrix $P$. This is derived by swapping the desired rows of the Identity Matrix.

To swap Row 1 and Row 2:
$$
P_{12} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
$$
Applied to a matrix:
$$
\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} c & d \\ a & b \end{bmatrix}
$$
*(Note: If you multiply by $P$ on the right, you swap columns).*

## 7. Inverse Matrices
How do we undo an elimination step?

If $E_{21}$ subtracted 3 * Row 1 from Row 2, the inverse $E_{21}^{-1}$ must **add** 3 * Row 1 back to Row 2.

$$
E_{21} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
\quad \xrightarrow{\text{Inverse}} \quad
E_{21}^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ \mathbf{3} & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

The product of a matrix and its inverse is the Identity Matrix:
$$ E^{-1} E = I $$
