The fundamental problem of linear algebra is to solve a system of $n$ linear equations with $n$ unknowns.

### I. Matrix Form: $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$

A system of linear equations is compactly written as a matrix-vector product equaling a vector:

$$\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$$

- $\mathbf{A}$: The **coefficient matrix** (rows are equations, columns are vectors).
    
- $\mathbf{x}$: The **vector of unknowns** (e.g., $\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$).
    
- $\mathbf{b}$: The **right-hand side vector**.
    

**Example (2x2 System):**

$$\begin{cases} 2x - y = 0 \\ -x + 2y = 3 \end{cases} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

---

### II. The Row Picture (Equation-at-a-time)

This geometric view looks at the graph of each equation.

#### 1. Two Dimensions ($n=2$)

- Each equation represents a **straight line** in the $xy$-plane.
    
- The **solution** $\mathbf{x}$ is the **point of intersection** of the lines.
    
    - _Example Solution:_ $x=1, y=2$ (the intersection point).
        

#### 2. Three Dimensions ($n=3$)

- Each equation represents a **plane** in $xyz$-space.
    
- The **solution** $\mathbf{x}$ is the **single point** where all three planes intersect.
    

**Note:** The row picture becomes difficult to visualize in higher dimensions.

---

### III. The Column Picture (Combination-at-a-time)

This is the most fundamental geometric interpretation. $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ asks: What linear combination of the columns of $\mathbf{A}$ equals the vector $\mathbf{b}$?

Matrix-Vector Multiplication ($A\mathbf{x}$):

The product of a matrix $\mathbf{A}$ and a vector $\mathbf{x}$ is a linear combination of the columns of $\mathbf{A}$.

$$A\mathbf{x} = x_1(\text{Column}_1) + x_2(\text{Column}_2) + \dots + x_n(\text{Column}_n)$$

#### 1. Example (2x2 System):

We seek $x$ and $y$ such that:

$$x \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} + y \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

- _Solution:_ We need $x=1$ and $y=2$ (the same solution as the row picture) to produce $\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$.
    

#### 2. Example (3x3 System):

Given the matrix $A$:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

We seek $x, y, z$ such that:

$$x \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} + y \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix} + z \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{b}$$

---

### IV. Solvability of $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$

The main question is: Can we solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ for **every** right-hand side $\mathbf{b}$?

- **Column Picture Interpretation:** This is equivalent to asking: **Do the linear combinations of the columns of $A$ fill the entire $n$-dimensional space?**
    

|**Case**|**Condition (Column Picture)**|**Matrix Type**|
|---|---|---|
|**Always Solvable**|The columns are "good" vectors whose combinations fill the entire space ($\mathbb{R}^n$).|**Non-singular** or **Invertible**|
|**Not Always Solvable**|The columns are "bad" (e.g., they all lie in a plane or subspace). Most $\mathbf{b}$ vectors will be unreachable.|**Singular** or **Not Invertible**|
